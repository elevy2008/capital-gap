The Missing Middle: Why Capital Skips Women’s Chronic Health

Sophia Blackwelder · Case study: endometriosis, with menopause and cardiovascular disease as comparisons

Women’s health has become a recognized investment category. What hasn’t followed is capital reaching the conditions that carry most of the burden. The money that does show up clusters almost entirely around three areas: cancers, reproductive health, and maternal health. Chronic, non-reproductive conditions that affect women across most of their adult lives remain, in investment terms, close to invisible. This issue asks why, using endometriosis as the primary case.

The shape of the gap

The World Economic Forum’s Women’s Health Investment Outlook, produced with the Boston Consulting Group and released in January 2026, puts a number on something long suspected: of the roughly $2.9 trillion in global private healthcare capital, only 6% goes toward conditions that specifically or disproportionately affect women.1 That alone is a familiar underfunding story. The more useful number is what happens inside that 6%: nearly 90% of it concentrates in cancers, reproductive health, and maternal health, the three categories investors have already learned to treat as investable markets.

That leaves roughly a tenth of an already small allocation, well under 1% of total private healthcare capital, to cover everything else the report flags as high-burden and under-addressed: cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, menopause, and Alzheimer’s in women, alongside conditions like endometriosis and PCOS. These aren’t rare diseases. They’re some of the most common conditions in women’s adult lives, and the same report estimates that addressing just four of them (cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, menopause, and Alzheimer’s) could unlock a market opportunity of over $100 billion in the U.S. alone by 2030.1

Case study: endometriosis

Endometriosis affects roughly one in ten women of reproductive age (an estimated 190 million people worldwide)2 and is a leading cause of chronic pelvic pain and infertility. It routinely takes years to diagnose.5 None of that is in dispute. What makes it a useful case study for capital allocation specifically is that the commercial case is unusually well quantified: McKinsey estimates the global commercial market for endometriosis treatment at $180–250 billion, a figure comparable to entire established therapeutic categories like cardiovascular disease or immunology.3

Despite that, capital hasn’t followed. A joint WEF–McKinsey Health Institute analysis found that between 2019 and 2023, startups working on endometriosis raised a combined $44 million globally, while startups working on erectile dysfunction, a much smaller addressable population, raised $1.24 billion in the same period.4 That’s not a funding gap in the usual sense of "underfunded relative to need." It’s a gap relative to a comparably sized, arguably better-quantified commercial opportunity in an adjacent category. The demand-side case for endometriosis is, if anything, stronger. The capital still isn’t there.

The pattern

The capital doesn’t stop at "women’s health is a hard sell." It stops earlier, at a specific structural point: conditions with a fragmented care pathway, no dominant specialty champion, and slow, imprecise diagnostics don’t get treated as investable markets, regardless of the size of the underlying opportunity.

Comparison: menopause

Menopause is a useful second case because the market-recognition problem, not the diagnostic problem, does most of the explaining. Every woman who lives past midlife goes through it, symptoms are well documented, and treatment categories, from hormone therapy to symptom-management devices, already exist. What’s been missing is investors treating it as a market at all, rather than a demographic inevitability outside the healthcare system’s commercial boundary. That recognition has started shifting only recently, which makes menopause a case of a capital gap closing in real time, useful as a contrast to endometriosis, where the diagnostic and care-pathway barriers are still largely unresolved.

Comparison: women’s cardiovascular health

Cardiovascular disease is the opposite kind of comparison: an enormous, thoroughly investable category overall, where the specific problem is that capital flows to the category without flowing to sex-specific presentation, diagnosis, or treatment. Heart disease is the leading cause of death for women,7 yet clinical research, diagnostic criteria, and product design have historically been calibrated to male presentation.6 Here the gap isn’t "no market." It’s a mainstream market that isn’t being asked to answer a sex-specific question, which is a different failure mode than endometriosis or menopause and points to a different fix: not new category creation, but better segmentation within an existing one.

Why the capital breaks down

Across the three cases, the same structural pattern shows up in different forms:

  • No specialty owns the patient. Reproductive and maternal health have obstetrics and gynecology as a clear front door that concentrates patient volume, data, and referral pathways. Endometriosis and menopause both cut across gynecology, pain management, and primary care, which fragments the data and the commercial pathway a startup would need to scale against.
  • Diagnostics lag the disease. Endometriosis still typically requires surgical laparoscopy for definitive diagnosis, and average time to diagnosis runs several years.5 Slow, invasive diagnostics compress the pipeline of investable companies before therapeutics-stage capital ever gets involved.
  • Thin data suppresses market sizing. Because research funding upstream has been thin, the epidemiological and outcomes data investors use to underwrite a market thesis is weaker for these conditions than for categories with decades of NIH-funded research behind them. That gap holds even where, as with endometriosis, the top-line market-size estimate is large.
  • Category precedent matters more than category size. Investors price new categories partly on comparable prior exits. Oncology and fertility both have that track record. Chronic, non-reproductive women’s health categories mostly don’t yet, which keeps them priced as unproven even when the underlying market math is comparable or better.

What would change it

Across the report, and across these three cases, the same lever keeps coming up: better data closes the gap faster than awareness campaigns do. Diagnostic tools that shorten time-to-diagnosis create the patient registries and outcomes data that make a market underwritable. That’s consistent with where the earliest capital is now showing up in menopause and starting to show up in endometriosis, not in awareness or advocacy but in diagnostics and data infrastructure that make the rest of the market investable behind them.
