Issue 03

The Farm Ownership Capital Gap: Why Is It Easier to Invest in Farmland Than to Become a Farmer?

Sophia Blackwelder · Case study: American farmland ownership and beginning-farmer finance

Farmland has become one of institutional finance’s favorite assets: finite supply, persistent demand, low correlation to public equities, and a century of appreciation.1 Capital has had no trouble recognizing that. What it hasn’t solved is financing the person who actually farms the land, and the clearest evidence of that gap is who owns American farmland today.

America’s most important family business

The popular image of American agriculture as dominated by faceless corporate operations doesn’t match the data. Family farms account for 96% of U.S. farm operations and 83% of total agricultural production.2 The family farm hasn’t disappeared. Its composition has shifted: a small share of large-scale family operations now produces nearly half of all U.S. output. The real question isn’t whether family farming survives. It already does. The question is whether the next generation will own these farms, or whether ownership keeps separating from operation.

Farmland: an asset the market loves

Farmland has characteristics few assets can match: it can’t be manufactured, its supply is fixed, and demand for food only grows with population. Pension funds, insurance companies, endowments, family offices, and dedicated farmland funds have all recognized this, treating farmland as a wealth-preservation asset with low correlation to public markets.1 Viewed only through that lens, agriculture looks well-capitalized.

But investing in farmland and becoming a farmer are different financial acts. An institutional investor buying farmland can lease it to an operator and collect rent and appreciation with limited operating risk. A farmer buying that same parcel takes on the appreciation and a capital-intensive operating business exposed to weather, commodity prices, and biology. Same asset, two completely different financial propositions. That’s why rising investor interest in farmland doesn’t automatically make ownership easier for the person who actually grows the food.

Becoming a farmer has never been more expensive

The barrier isn’t just land price. It’s the cumulative cost of financing an entire enterprise before any revenue arrives: machinery, seed, fertilizer, livestock, fuel, irrigation, insurance, and labor, all paid for months before harvest. Illustrative enterprise-cost estimates for establishing a modern grain operation (land, machinery, storage, and working capital) run into the millions of dollars, though actual costs vary enormously by geography, crop, and scale. A farmer isn’t only buying real estate. They’re simultaneously financing a real estate acquisition and an operating company that can’t pause production when conditions turn: seeds go in on a seasonal clock regardless of prices, and livestock need care regardless of margins.

The beginning farmer problem

The USDA defines a beginning farmer as someone who has operated for 10 years or less, and consistently identifies the same three barriers facing them: access to land, access to capital, and access to knowledge.7 Those barriers reinforce each other. No land means no collateral. No collateral means no financing. No operating history means lenders price in more risk. The federal government has built specialized programs around exactly this gap, including the Farm Service Agency’s Farm Ownership Loans and Beginning Farmer Down Payment Loan Program.8 Their existence is itself the evidence: if conventional lending served beginning farmers adequately, purpose-built government loan programs wouldn’t be necessary.

Ownership without operation

This is where the gap becomes visible in the data. Roughly 39% of U.S. farm and ranch land is rented rather than owner-operated. Of that rented land, about 80% is owned by landlords who don’t farm it themselves.3 And per USDA’s most recent land-tenure survey, 52% of the individuals who principally own rented-out farmland have never farmed at all, a figure that has been rising as ownership increasingly passes to heirs, trusts, and investors rather than working farmers.

Leasing itself isn’t the problem. For many beginning farmers, it’s the only realistic way into the industry, letting them farm at scale without the debt of buying land outright. The problem is what it does to wealth-building over time. Agricultural production creates value every season. Land ownership is what captures that value as long-term net worth. A tenant farmer can operate highly productive land for decades while capturing very little of its appreciation, a quiet but structural barrier to the next generation ever accumulating enough equity to buy in.

Succession is a financing problem

Farm succession gets framed as a family issue. It’s equally a balance-sheet issue. Industry surveys consistently find that a majority of American farmers (estimates range from roughly two-thirds to 70%) have no written succession or estate plan.9 That matters because a modern farm is usually the family’s largest asset: land, equipment, livestock, and grain storage can represent millions of dollars that need to move across generations without either side making an unsustainable financial decision. Parents may need the farm’s value to fund retirement. Only one child may want to keep farming, and that child may not have the capital to buy out siblings at market value. None of that is a failure of interest in farming. It’s a failure of financing mechanisms built to bridge exactly this kind of transfer.

The ownership paradox

An institutional investor buying farmland wants appreciation, inflation protection, and rental income. A farmer buying the same parcel takes on all of that plus weather risk, commodity-price volatility, and a capital-intensive operating business. Same land, two entirely different financial propositions. That’s why markets have no trouble pricing farmland as an asset while still leaving beginning farmers with no realistic way to own it.

The Farm Ownership Capital Gap

None of this means institutional ownership is displacing family farming, or that investor capital in farmland is inherently harmful. That would oversimplify a more complicated system. The actual finding is narrower and more structural: American agriculture has a mismatch between the capital willing to buy farmland and the capital willing to help farmers own and operate it. The market has already priced the asset correctly. It hasn’t built financing built around the operator.

From financing land to financing farmers

The useful question isn’t "how do we attract more capital into agriculture." Agriculture already attracts plenty. It’s "how does existing capital better reach the people who operate the land." USDA beginning-farmer loan programs and Farm Credit’s specialized agricultural lending already address part of this. Seller financing, gradual ownership transfers, conservation easements, cooperative ownership, and shared-equity models are all mechanisms worth scaling, not because any one of them is a universal fix (a Midwest grain operation and a California vineyard have almost nothing in common financially), but because financial innovation in agriculture has overwhelmingly focused on financing the land. The open opportunity is financing the operator with the same sophistication.

Conclusion

Family farms still account for 96% of U.S. farm operations and 83% of production.2 Farmland still draws sophisticated capital seeking appreciation and diversification. And becoming a farmer has still never required more money, with beginning producers facing real barriers to land, operating capital, and long-term wealth-building. The explanation isn’t a shortage of capital in agriculture. It’s that capital increasingly flows toward farmland as an asset while ownership stays out of reach for the people who work it. Markets have already decided farmland is valuable. The unresolved question is whether financing will ever treat the farmer the same way.

Methodology and scope

This issue draws on USDA Economic Research Service, Farm Service Agency, and National Agricultural Statistics Service data (including the 2022 Census of Agriculture and the 2024 TOTAL land-tenure survey), industry research on farmland as an institutional asset class, and farm-succession industry surveys. It focuses on the relationship between farmland ownership, beginning-farmer finance, and succession, and distinguishes throughout between owning agricultural land and operating an agricultural business as economically distinct activities. Where cited figures could not be independently verified as originally stated, they were corrected against the primary USDA data above, notably the share of U.S. farms that are family-owned and their share of production, and the farmland succession-planning estimate, which industry surveys put in a range rather than a single precise figure.
